Background

How it works

G3c in a nutshell

G3c is a sequent calculus for classical propositional logic. A sequent is written Γ ⇒ Δ, where Γ (the antecedent) is a list of assumptions and Δ (the succedent) is a list of conclusions. The sequent holds when at least one formula on the left is false or at least one on the right is true.

What makes G3c different from its relatives (like G1c or Gentzen's original LK) is that weakening and contraction are built into the logical rules rather than existing as separate structural rules. That's what "contraction-free" means here. For proof search, this is convenient because there are fewer rule choices to worry about at each step.

The rules

Two axiom forms and eight logical rules (one left and one right rule per connective). Reading top to bottom: the premise(s) are above the line, the conclusion is below.

Why the search always terminates

The algorithm applies rules backward, starting from the goal sequent at the bottom and working up toward axioms. Each backward step replaces a compound formula with its immediate subformulas.

To see why this can't go on forever, define the depth of a formula recursively: atoms have depth 0, and each connective adds 1. The depth of a sequent is the sum of the depths of all its formula occurrences. Every backward rule application strictly decreases this number. Since it's a natural number, it has to hit zero eventually, at which point every formula is atomic and we can check whether we've reached an axiom or a dead end.

Because G3c is already cut-free, we also get the subformula property automatically: every formula that shows up in the proof is a subformula of what you typed in. The search never wanders outside that finite set.

Context

About

This was built as a final project for PHIL 579 (Proof Theory), Winter 2026, at the University of Calgary. The course is taught by Professor Richard Zach.

The proof search implements backward chaining through the propositional fragment of G3c as defined in the course textbook. Termination follows from the observation that each backward rule application strictly reduces sequent depth, and the subformula property ensures only subformulas of the input ever appear.
